Compostable vs Recyclable Coffee Cups: A UK Café Guide

"Compostable", "recyclable", "biodegradable" — the labels sound interchangeable, and cup suppliers rarely explain the difference honestly. Here's what each one actually means, what happens to the cup after your customer walks out, and which is the right call for a UK café in 2026.

AC Supply Co. · Updated July 2026
The short answer

For most UK cafés that don't control where the cup ends up, a good-quality recyclable paper cup collected through a dedicated cup-takeback scheme is the more honest, lower-impact choice today. The UK still has very little industrial composting capacity, so a compostable cup usually ends up incinerated or in landfill anyway — and if a customer drops it in the wrong bin, it can contaminate paper recycling.

Compostable cups genuinely win in closed settings where you control disposal — festivals, stadiums, canteens, event catering — because you can guarantee they reach an industrial composter. Reusable-cup schemes beat both where your model supports them.

The three words, in plain English

Most of the confusion comes from treating three different claims as if they mean the same thing. They don't, and the gap between them matters for what you buy.

TermWhat it actually promisesThe catch
Recyclable Can be reprocessed into new material if it reaches the right facility. The thin plastic lining means it needs a specialist plant, not the kerbside bin.
Compostable Breaks down into safe, non-toxic matter under the right conditions. "The right conditions" usually means an industrial composter — not your food-waste caddy or garden.
Biodegradable Will break down eventually. No promised timescale and no guarantee it leaves nothing harmful behind. The weakest claim of the three.

The headline point: "biodegradable" on its own is close to meaningless as an environmental claim. Almost anything is biodegradable given enough time. If a supplier is leaning on that word rather than "recyclable" or "certified compostable", treat it as marketing, not substance.

Why "compostable" isn't the automatic win people assume

Most compostable cups are lined with PLA, a bioplastic made from corn starch or sugarcane. On paper it's lovely. In practice, PLA only breaks down properly in a controlled industrial composting environment held at the right temperature — it won't meaningfully decompose in landfill, and it isn't designed for a home compost heap.

The problem is infrastructure. The UK has very few commercial composting facilities that accept packaging like this, and most household food-waste collections don't want compostable cups in the caddy. So the compostable cup that felt like the responsible choice frequently ends up in exactly the same place as a standard one: incinerated or landfilled. Worse, if a well-meaning customer puts it in the paper recycling, a PLA-lined cup can contaminate the batch.

None of this means compostable cups are a con — it means the cup is only as good as the system that collects it. Buy compostable when you can guarantee that system exists.

What actually happens to a recyclable cup

Standard takeaway cups are paperboard with a very thin polyethylene lining that stops them leaking. That lining is what makes them awkward: recycling them requires a specialist facility that can separate the paper fibre from the plastic, and the cups have to be collected through a dedicated cup-recovery stream rather than the general recycling bin.

Where that collection exists, the outcome is genuinely good — the recovered paper pulp gets a second life in products like napkins and packaging. Where it doesn't, a "recyclable" cup in the normal bin is usually screened out and sent to landfill, the same as any other. This is the uncomfortable truth the label hides: on its own, "recyclable" only describes what's possible, not what happens.

That's why the most effective thing a café can do is less about the cup and more about the collection — signing up to a cup-takeback or recycling scheme, and pointing customers at front-of-house cup bins rather than assuming kerbside will sort it out.

Which bin do compostable cups go in?

Short version: not your mixed recycling, and usually not your food-waste caddy either. Compostable cups need a dedicated commercial composting collection to do what they promise. Unless your local authority or waste contractor specifically accepts compostable packaging — most still don't — the cup will be treated as general waste. If you're going to stock compostable, sort the collection contract first; otherwise you're paying a premium for a cup that behaves like a standard one at the end of its life.

So which should your café choose?

Here's our honest take as a supplier, rather than the answer that simply sells the most expensive cup:

  • High-street café or takeaway, no control over disposal: a good-quality recyclable paper cup, ideally paired with a cup-takeback scheme, is the pragmatic choice. It's cheaper than compostable, and without industrial composting access the compostable premium buys little real-world benefit.
  • Closed venue — event space, stadium, festival, workplace canteen: compostable comes into its own, because you can route every cup to an industrial composter. This is the scenario compostable packaging was designed for.
  • Sit-in or loyal regulars: a reusable-cup or deposit scheme beats every disposable option on impact and, over time, on cost. Keep disposables for genuine takeaway.
  • Brand-led business that wants the sustainability story: whichever you choose, back it with a real collection process and honest signage. A compostable cup with nowhere to compost it is a story that doesn't survive scrutiny.

The infrastructure is slowly improving, and this advice will shift as more industrial composting and cup-recovery capacity comes online. But for a typical UK café buying today, we'd steer you toward recyclable-plus-collection over compostable-in-hope.

A note on cost and supply

Compostable cups generally carry a higher unit cost than standard recyclable paper cups, and the price gap widens once you add lids and sleeves in matching materials. If sustainability is core to your brand, that premium can be well worth it — but only if the disposal side is sorted, otherwise you're paying more for the same end result.
